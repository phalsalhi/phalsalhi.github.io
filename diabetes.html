Diabetes Classification at a Glance

TYPE 1
  • Mechanism: Autoimmune β-cell destruction → absolute insulinopenia
  • Onset: Any age; most common DM in children
  • Markers: Positive islet autoantibodies (GAD, IA-2, ZnT8)
  • Key risk: DKA, severe hypoglycemia, neurocognitive effects
  • Treatment cornerstone: Insulin (MDI or CSII/AID)
TYPE 2
  • Mechanism: Progressive β-cell dysfunction + insulin resistance
  • Onset: Adults; increasing in youth especially marginalized groups
  • Features: Obesity common; autoantibodies negative
  • Key risk: ASCVD, CKD, HF, MASLD, microvascular complications
  • Treatment: Lifestyle + pharmacotherapy; insulin when needed
OVERLAP / DIAGNOSTIC PITFALLS
  • In obese youth: T1D and T2D presentations overlap — check autoantibodies
  • MODY: Monogenic; consider if atypical features, family history
  • CFRD: Requires insulin; A1C unreliable for monitoring
  • Insulin resistance + T1D ("double diabetes"): Both mechanisms can coexist

⚖️ Basal–Prandial Ratio & Dosing
  • Typical split: 30–50% basal, 50–70% prandial
  • Starting dose (adults): 0.4–0.5 units/kg/day (metabolically stable)
  • New diagnosis: 0.2–0.6 units/kg/day; lower in honeymoon
  • Puberty / illness / late luteal phase: Higher doses required
  • Adjustment steps: 1–4 units or 5–10% per change
  • Fasting hyperglycemia: Adjust basal insulin (review overnight CGM)
  • Postprandial hyperglycemia: Adjust prandial timing, ICR, or bolus technique
  • Overbasalization signs: High bedtime→morning differential, unexplained hypoglycemia, high glycemic variability

Technology & Monitoring

CGM — Standard of Care for T1D
  • Who: All T1D patients — initiate at or soon after diagnosis
  • Metrics to review: TIR, TBR (<70 and <54 mg/dL), TAR (>180 and >250 mg/dL), CV, GMI
  • Minimum wear: ≥70% active time over 14 days (≥7 days acceptable in children)
  • TIR–A1C correlation: TIR >70% ≈ A1C ~7%
  • CV >36%: High hypoglycemia risk signal
AID — Automated Insulin Delivery
  • Components: CGM + insulin pump + control algorithm
  • Proven benefits: ↑TIR, ↓A1C, ↓hypoglycemia vs MDI and conventional pump
  • Indication: Preferred for all T1D who can use safely (independently or with caregiver)
  • Hybrid closed-loop: Automates basal; user still boluses for meals
  • Note: Carb ratio, ISF, and targets can usually be adjusted within system

✅
Core Principle For T2D with ASCVD, HF, or CKD — initiate GLP-1 RA and/or SGLT2i regardless of A1C level. These agents provide cardiovascular/kidney benefit beyond glucose lowering and their initiation should not be delayed until glycemic failure.

Insulin Initiation in T2D

When to Start Insulin
  • A1C >10% or blood glucose ≥300 mg/dL → start insulin immediately
  • Symptoms of hyperglycemia (polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss)
  • Signs of catabolism
  • Diagnosis of T1D uncertain at onset
  • Failure of GLP-1 RA/oral therapy to achieve goal
Basal Insulin — Starting & Titrating
  • Start: 10 units/day OR 0.1–0.2 units/kg/day
  • Titration: +2 units every 3 days targeting FPG goal without hypoglycemia
  • Preferred basal: LAA (glargine, degludec) over NPH — less nocturnal hypoglycemia
  • U-300 glargine / degludec: Even lower nocturnal hypoglycemia risk
  • Overbasalization: Bedtime→morning differential ≥50 mg/dL; add prandial insulin or GLP-1 RA
  • Pre-prandial step-up: 4 units or 10% of basal at largest meal first
💡
GLP-1 RA Before Prandial Insulin When A1C remains above goal on basal insulin, add a GLP-1 RA (or dual GIP/GLP-1 RA) BEFORE adding prandial insulin. Comparable or superior A1C reduction with less hypoglycemia, better weight outcomes, and cardiovascular benefits.

Hypoglycemia — Classification & Management

Level 1 Alert Value
54–70
mg/dL (3.0–3.9 mmol/L)
  • Clinically important even if asymptomatic
  • Triggers counterregulatory response
  • Symptoms: shakiness, sweating, hunger, tachycardia
  • Action: Treat with 15g fast carbs
Level 2 Clinically Significant
<54
mg/dL (<3.0 mmol/L)
  • Threshold for neuroglycopenic symptoms
  • Requires immediate action
  • If asymptomatic → suspect impaired hypoglycemia awareness
  • Triggers: Full reevaluation of treatment plan
Level 3 Severe Event
Any
glucose level — altered status
  • Altered mental and/or physical function
  • Requires assistance from another person
  • Risk of coma, seizure, death
  • Action: Glucagon + emergency services if unconscious
🍬 Hypoglycemia Treatment Protocol — "15–15 Rule"
1
BG ≤70 mg/dL — Conscious Patient
Ingest 15g fast-acting carbohydrates containing glucose (glucose tablets preferred). Avoid high-fat/protein foods — they delay absorption. Acarbose users: must use pure glucose only.
2
Wait 15 Minutes → Recheck BG
If still ≤70 mg/dL, repeat 15g glucose. AID system users: typically only 5–10g carbs needed (algorithm already reducing insulin).
3
Unconscious / Unable to Swallow → Glucagon
Administer prescribed glucagon. Intranasal (3mg) or ready-to-inject preferred over reconstituted powder. Call emergency services. NEVER give insulin in this situation.
4
After Recovery — Prevent Recurrence
Identify precipitant. Document event. Reevaluate treatment plan at next visit. One or more Level 2–3 events → deintensify therapy.

Hypoglycemia Risk Factors

Major Risk Factors (independent, strong association)
  • Recent Level 2–3 event (within 3–6 months)
  • Intensive insulin therapy (MDI, CSII, AID)
  • Impaired hypoglycemia awareness (unawareness)
  • Kidney failure
  • Cognitive impairment or dementia
  • History of metabolic/bariatric surgery
  • Food insecurity
  • Housing instability
  • Low income / underinsurance
  • Fasting for religious reasons (without appropriate plan)
Other Risk Factors (less consistent evidence)
  • Recurrent Level 1 events
  • Basal insulin therapy
  • Age ≥75 years
  • Female sex
  • High glycemic variability (CV >36%)
  • Polypharmacy
  • CVD, CKD (eGFR <60), neuropathy, retinopathy
  • Major depressive disorder / severe mental illness
  • Gastroparesis
  • β-Blocker therapy (masks adrenergic symptoms)
  • Alcohol / substance use disorder
  • Low health literacy

GLP-1 RA — Key Side Effects & Practical Tips
  • GI effects (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea): Most common; reduce meal size, slow dose titration; avoid high-fat/spicy food
  • Pancreatitis: Rare association; discontinue if suspected; avoid in high-risk patients
  • Biliary disease: Monitor for gallstones; evaluate if symptoms arise
  • Pre-operative: Withhold before procedures with general anesthesia/deep sedation (pulmonary aspiration risk)
  • Oral contraceptives: Tirzepatide alters OCP levels at initiation/titration → use backup contraception for ≥4 weeks at maintenance dose
  • Thyroid C-cell tumors: Rodent data; avoid in personal/family history of medullary thyroid cancer or MEN2
  • NAION: Rare; monitor eyes, especially at high CVD risk
  • Do NOT combine GLP-1 RA + DPP-4i: No additive benefit; redundant mechanism

T1D in Children & Adolescents

ℹ️
Key Difference from Adults Pediatric T1D management requires unique consideration of developmental stage, family dynamics, neurological vulnerability to hypo/hyperglycemia, school environments, and the emerging independence of adolescence. Care plans cannot simply be derived from adult protocols.
🎯 Glycemic Goals — Pediatric T1D
  • A1C <7% (<53 mmol/mol): Goal for most children/adolescents
  • A1C <6.5% (<48 mmol/mol): If achievable without adverse effects; consider with AID access
  • Less stringent goals: Hypoglycemia unawareness, cannot articulate symptoms, no CGM/advanced tech access
  • Even less stringent: Severe hypoglycemia history, limited life expectancy
  • Honeymoon phase: Lower goals may be feasible
  • Children <6 years: Particularly vulnerable — registry data show lower A1C achievable without ↑severe hypo with modern tech
📡 Technology Recommendations
  • CGM: Offer at diagnosis or as soon as possible — Rec. A
  • AID systems: Offer to all who can use safely (self or with caregiver) — Rec. A
  • Insulin pump (open-loop): Offer if AID not accessible, on MDI — Rec. A
  • School support: Students must be supported in using all diabetes tech as prescribed — Rec. C
  • CGM data: Use ≥14-day data (≥7 days may be sufficient for children at goal)
Exercise Management in Pediatric T1D
🏃
Target BG Before Exercise: 126–180 mg/dL (7.0–10.0 mmol/L). Individualize based on insulin plan and activity type/duration.
  • Postpone intense activity if: BG ≥350 mg/dL + moderate/large ketones or β-hydroxybutyrate >1.5 mmol/L + insulin deficiency
  • Caution if: β-hydroxybutyrate ≥0.6 mmol/L without insulin
  • Hypoglycemia nadir: Typically 8–16h post-exercise; nocturnal hypoglycemia risk ↑ with prolonged activity
  • Carbohydrate for aerobic exercise (30–60 min, fasting): 10–15g pre-exercise
  • After insulin bolus: 0.5–1.0g carbs/kg/hour of exercise
  • Insulin pump (no AID): Reduce basal rate 10–50% or suspend 1–2h during exercise
  • After exercise: Reduce basal/long-acting by ~20% to prevent delayed hypoglycemia
  • AID systems: Use activity/exercise mode or raise glucose target before activity; carb intake timing matters (AI may ↑ insulin in response to pre-exercise carb spike)
  • Goal activity: 60 min/day moderate-to-vigorous aerobic; muscle/bone-strengthening ≥3 days/week

T2D in Children & Adolescents

⚠️
Youth-Onset T2D is More Aggressive Than Adult-Onset T2D Faster β-cell decline, earlier microvascular/macrovascular complications, higher rates of hypertension and nephropathy in young adulthood (TODAY study). Target A1C <6.5% (lower than adult T1D goal) is justified by lower hypoglycemia risk and higher complication burden.
Initial Treatment — Based on Presentation
PresentationInitial TreatmentThen
A1C <8.5%, no acidosis, no ketosis (incidental diagnosis) Metformin (titrate to 2,000 mg/day as tolerated) Check autoantibodies; if negative → continue metformin; if glycemic goals not met → add GLP-1 RA or SGLT2i
A1C ≥8.5%, no acidosis, ± ketosis Metformin + long-acting insulin (start 0.5 units/kg/day, titrate every 2–3 days) Check autoantibodies; if negative → titrate insulin + metformin; prioritize non-insulin meds to minimize weight gain; if autoab+ → manage as T1D
Acidosis / DKA / HHS IV insulin until acidosis resolves; then subcutaneous insulin; manage DKA/HHS per protocol Add metformin once ketosis resolves; check autoantibodies; adjust therapy once metabolically stable
Approved Drug Classes in Youth T2D
  • Metformin: First-line for A1C <8.5%; titrate to 2,000 mg/day
  • GLP-1 RAs: Liraglutide, exenatide ER, dulaglutide — FDA-approved for youth; effective A1C ↓ + weight loss
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: Empagliflozin, dapagliflozin, canagliflozin — approved age 10–17 years; proven A1C reduction in RCTs
  • Insulin: Required for DKA/HHS; consider pump for long-term MDI users
  • Tirzepatide: Phase 3 trial (SURPASS-PEDS) shows significant A1C + weight reduction at 5 and 10 mg weekly — no regulatory approval yet in pediatrics
Metabolic Surgery in Adolescents
  • Indication: Class 2 obesity (BMI 35–<40 kg/m² or 120–<140% of 95th percentile) with elevated A1C or serious comorbidities despite lifestyle + pharmacotherapy
  • OR: BMI ≥40 kg/m² with or without comorbidities
  • Results: Similar weight loss to adults, higher T2D and hypertension remission rates
  • 10-year Teen-LABS data: Durable weight loss and comorbidity remission
  • Most common procedure: Vertical sleeve gastrectomy
  • Requirement: Experienced surgeon + full interprofessional team (surgeon, endocrinologist, dietitian, behavioral health, nurse)

Psychosocial Care & Transition

Psychosocial Screening — When & What
  • Diabetes distress: ~30% of T1D youth, ~24% of T2D youth; screen from age 7–8 using validated tools
  • Depression: Prevalence 22.2% in pediatric DM; screen from age 12
  • Anxiety: Prevalence 17.7%; screen from age 8
  • Disordered eating: Screen when unexplained weight loss, hyperglycemia, DKA, or behavioral cues present; insulin omission for weight loss is dangerous (50.3% of T2D youth report disordered eating in SEARCH)
  • Fear of hypoglycemia: Screen when severe/frequent hypo; children as young as 6 can self-report
  • Social adjustment & school performance: Ask at each visit
Transition to Adult Care
  • Begin transition preparation: Early adolescence; at minimum 1 year before transfer
  • No age cutoff: Transfer is collaborative, readiness-based — not age-driven
  • High-risk period: Emerging adulthood — lapses in care, deteriorating glycemic control, ↑DKA, psychosocial challenges
  • Strategies: Transition coordinators, technology-supported communication, peer support, overlap period with adult and pediatric teams
  • Team needed: Clinician + CDCES + nurse + behavioral health + dietitian + social worker
  • U.S. Affordable Care Act: Coverage until age 26 under parent's plan

DKA & HHS — Recognition & Risk Reduction

🚨
Hyperglycemic Crises Are Life-Threatening & Increasing Rates of DKA and HHS have increased over the past decade in both T1D and T2D. The majority of DKA in established diabetes arises from missed/insufficient insulin, not new-onset diabetes. Always screen for underlying psychosocial triggers.
Risk Factors for Hyperglycemic Crises
  • Type 1 diabetes / absolute insulin deficiency
  • Younger age (particularly age 10–14)
  • Prior history of DKA or HHS
  • Prior hypoglycemic crises
  • High A1C or elevated daily insulin dose/kg
  • Insulin rationing / cost-related non-adherence
  • SGLT2 inhibitor use (especially T1D)
  • Behavioral health conditions (depression, eating disorders, bipolar)
  • Alcohol or substance use
  • Social determinants: food/housing insecurity, public insurance
  • Transition period from pediatric to adult care
  • COVID-19 infection (new-onset T1D presentations)
  • Glucocorticoids, antipsychotics, immune checkpoint inhibitors
Cannabis Hyperemesis & DKA Differentiation (T1D)
  • Hyperglycemic ketosis-cannabis hyperemesis syndrome: Nausea → severe vomiting → ketosis + alkalosis → hyperglycemia
  • Key differentiation from DKA: pH ≥7.4 and bicarbonate >15 mmol/L in presence of ketosis
  • Cannabis use rates in T1D adolescents: 17–31% in various studies
  • Management: Treat underlying DM emergency; address cannabis use; routine care should discourage recreational cannabis
Sick Day Self-Management for DKA Prevention
  • NEVER stop basal insulin during illness
  • Monitor blood ketones (β-hydroxybutyrate) — superior to urine ketones (50% ↓ hospitalization in RCT)
  • Check ketones if BG >200 mg/dL or symptoms present
  • Mild DKA at home: hydration + subcutaneous insulin if hemodynamically stable + able to tolerate fluids
  • Seek immediate care if: unable to keep fluids down, ongoing vomiting, ketones not clearing, altered mental status

Nutrition Notes
  • Do not restrict below 0.8 g/kg/day in CKD + diabetes — does not alter glucose, CV risk, or GFR decline

  • Sodium: restrict to <2,300 mg/day for BP management and CV risk reduction
  • Potassium: individualize based on labs, comorbidities, and medications

  • A1C target of ~7% for CKD prevention/slowing in both T1D and T2D

AKI, Pregnancy & Special Situations

Acute Kidney Injury in Diabetes
  • People with diabetes have higher risk of AKI than those without diabetes
  • AKI hospitalization rate is 5× higher in adults with diabetes (RR 5.0)
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: earlier concerns about volume-depletion AKI have NOT been confirmed in RCTs of advanced CKD or high CVD risk
  • nsMRAs (finerenone): do not increase AKI risk when used for kidney protection
  • AKI is associated with increased risks of progressive CKD and other poor outcomes — identify and treat promptly
💡
Creatinine Rise on RAS Blockers is NOT AKI: The ACCORD BP trial demonstrated that up to 30% creatinine increase with intensive BP lowering did NOT increase mortality or progressive kidney disease. Continue RAS blockade for ≤30% creatinine rise without volume depletion.

🔬 On Admission — Required Actions
  • A1C on admission for all patients with diabetes or hyperglycemia (>140 mg/dL) if no result in prior 3 months Rec 16.1 B

  • Implement validated written or computerized provider order entry (CPOE) sets for dysglycemia management Rec 16.2 B

  • Systematic reviews show: better time in range, lower mean glucose, no increase in hypoglycemia with computerised decision support

  • Team may include: physicians, NPs, PAs, nurses, dietitians, pharmacists

Glucose Monitoring in Hospital

📍 Point-of-Care (POC) Monitoring
  • Eating patients: Before each meal
  • NPO / not eating: Every 4–6 hours
  • IV insulin infusion: Every 30 min to 2 hours
  • Use FDA-approved hospital-calibrated glucose monitoring systems
  • POC less accurate than lab analyzers but adequate for usual practice
  • Verify any POC result discordant with clinical status: repeat POC → lab if still discordant (especially for asymptomatic hypoglycemia)
  • Error sources: perfusion abnormalities, edema, anemia, certain medications
📲 CGM in the Hospital B
  • Continue personal CGM during hospitalization when clinically appropriate Rec 16.6 B
  • Confirmatory POC still required for insulin dosing decisions
  • CGM validity criterion: within ±20% of POC when glucose ≥70 mg/dL, or within ±20 mg/dL when <70 mg/dL ("20/20 criterion")
  • No CGM device yet FDA-approved for inpatient use (enforcement discretion during COVID-19)
  • CGM advantages: nocturnal hypoglycemia detection, impending hypoglycemia alerts, hybrid IV insulin protocols reducing hourly POC burden
  • Recent RCT (TIGHT study, type 2 DM, non-critical): CGM showed no benefit vs POC for mean glucose or hypoglycemia rates
⚙️ AID / Insulin Pump in Hospital C
  • Continue insulin pump or AID if clinically appropriate Rec 16.7 C
  • Patient must be willing and able to self-manage; requires staff training
  • POC monitoring still required: 1–6 times/day for AID users
  • AID requires functional CGM → verify 20/20 criterion at least daily
  • Review CGM and AID data daily; adjust pump settings as needed
  • RCT in T2D (non-critical): fully closed-loop better glycemia vs standard SC insulin, no extra hypoglycemia risk

Insulin Therapy — Inpatient Protocols

ADA Recommendations — Insulin in Hospital
16.8a Continuous IV insulin infusion is recommended for achieving glycemic goals in critically ill individuals. Use validated protocols with predefined adjustments based on glycemic fluctuations. A
16.8b Basal insulin or basal + correction plan is preferred for noncritically ill patients with poor/no oral intake. A
16.9 Basal + prandial + correction (basal-bolus) is preferred for most noncritically ill patients with adequate nutritional intake. A
16.10 Correction/supplemental insulin alone (sliding scale) without basal is discouraged in the inpatient setting for most patients. Exception: T2D non-critical care with mild hyperglycemia maintaining BG <180 mg/dL. A
💊 Total Daily Dose (TDD) Calculation
  • Weight-based start: 0.3–0.6 units/kg/day
  • From IV infusion: Use last 6–8h stable rate; apply ~60% to account for glucotoxicity
  • From home insulin: Use 80% of home dose
  • Split: 50% as basal, 50% as nutritional (prandial)
  • Correction scale: Low (<40 units/day TDD), Medium (40–80), High (>80)
  • Correction insulin starts at 140–150 mg/dL (7.8–8.3 mmol/L)
🔄 IV → Subcutaneous Transition
  • Give basal SC insulin 2 hours before stopping IV infusion (prevents rebound hyperglycemia)
  • Early addition of low-dose basal analog (0.15–0.3 units/kg) during IV therapy may reduce IV duration and prevent rebound — especially useful in T1D
  • TDD calculation options: weight-based, prior home dose, or last 6–8h infusion rate
  • Use insulin pen or meticulous pharmacy/nursing supervision for dose accuracy
⚠️ Special Considerations
  • Type 1 DM: Sliding scale alone is contraindicated. Always maintain basal insulin even if NPO. EHR alerts should prevent basal insulin being held in T1D.
  • Insulin analogs vs human insulin: Similar glycemic outcomes; human insulin → higher hypoglycemia risk
  • Premixed insulins (70/30, 75/25, 50/50): Not recommended for inpatient use — higher hypoglycemia vs basal-bolus
  • Concentrated insulins (U-300, U-500): Limited inpatient data; U-500 users may need 75% dose reduction; pen preferred to reduce errors

⚠️ Inpatient Hypoglycemia — Risk Factors & Prevention
Risk Factors
  • Kidney failure (↓ insulin clearance)
  • Advanced age, heart failure, liver failure
  • Sepsis, malnutrition
  • Insulin dosing / timing errors
  • Missed dose follow-up after first episode

  • Hospital-wide nurse-initiated hypoglycemia protocol for BG <70 mg/dL Rec 16.12 A/C

📋 Preoperative Goals
  • A1C goal <8% within 3 months before elective surgery Rec 16.14 C

  • Do not delay required surgery based on A1C alone — may harm patient
  • Preoperative optimization programs shown to improve perioperative glycemia and reduce hospital stay

Transition to Outpatient — Discharge Planning

16.18 A structured discharge plan tailored to the individual with diabetes reduces length of stay, readmission rates, and improves satisfaction. Begin planning at admission; update as needs change. B

  • Cross-check home and hospital medications — no duplication, no inadvertent stopping of chronic meds
  • Fill and review new/changed prescriptions with patient at or before discharge
  • Ensure access to medications, supplies, and durable medical equipment
  • Provide CGM sensors, test strips, insulin pens, pen needles, syringes, lancets as needed

  • Transmit discharge summary to primary care provider promptly
  • Communicate: medication changes, pending results, follow-up needs
  • Schedule outpatient follow-up before discharge — increases attendance
  • Follow-up within 1 month for all patients with inpatient hyperglycemia/hypoglycemia
  • Follow-up in 1–2 weeks if medications changed or glycemia not optimal at discharge

Section 13 — ADA Standards of Care 2026 Over 29% of people aged ≥65 years have diabetes. Diabetes in older adults is highly heterogeneous — ranging from healthy individuals with intact function to those with frailty, dementia, and end-stage illness. Neurocognitive Function

13.3 Screening for early detection of mild cognitive impairment or dementia should be performed for adults ≥65 years at the initial visit, annually, and as appropriate. B
🔗 Diabetes–Cognition Link
  • Higher incidence of all-cause dementia, Alzheimer disease, and vascular dementia
  • Both hyperglycemia and hypoglycemia → cognitive decline
  • Longer diabetes duration → worsening cognitive function
  • Severe hypoglycemia linked to increased risk of dementia (bidirectional)
  • Diabetes-related dementia: emerging distinct entity — slower progression, no typical neuroimaging findings, high insulin use, frailty, sarcopenia, dynapenia
🛠 Assessment Tools
  • Mini-Cog — 5 items, 3 min, multiple languages. Cut-off <3
  • MMSE — Mini-Mental State Examination
  • MoCA — Montreal Cognitive Assessment (sensitive for mild impairment)
  • Refer to behavioral health for formal neuropsychological evaluation if screening positive and feasible
⚡ Clinical Triggers for Screening
  • Errors in calculating insulin dose
  • Difficulty counting carbohydrates
  • Skipped meals or insulin doses
  • Difficulty recognizing / preventing / treating hypoglycemia
  • Significant decline in self-care activities or medication management
⚠️
When Cognitive Dysfunction Is Identified Simplify care plans and engage appropriate support structures. Complex self-care tasks (glucose monitoring, insulin dosing, meal timing) become high-risk. GLP-1 RAs are associated with a statistically significant reduction in all-cause dementia risk. Management of blood pressure and statin use are also associated with reduced risk of incident dementia.

Hypoglycemia in Older Adults

13.4 Ascertain and address episodes of hypoglycemia at routine visits — older adults have greater risk, especially when treated with sulfonylureas, meglitinides, and insulin. B
13.5 Recommend CGM for older adults with T1D A and T2D on insulin therapy B to improve glycemic outcomes, reduce hypoglycemia, and reduce treatment burden.
13.6 Consider automated insulin delivery (AID) systems B and connected pens E to reduce hypoglycemia risk in older adults, based on individual ability and support system.
🚨 Why Older Adults Are at Higher Risk
  • Irregular meal intake, particularly with insulin
  • Worsening kidney function → drug accumulation
  • Cognitive impairment → difficulty with self-care & recognition
  • Slowed hormonal counter-regulation
  • Polypharmacy interactions (esp. fluoroquinolones + SUs)
  • Suboptimal hydration and variable nutritional intake
📡 CGM in Older Adults — Evidence
  • Effective across all age groups including T1D and insulin-requiring T2D
  • Benefits also shown for non-insulin users (reduces hypoglycemia)
  • May take longer to learn — engage caregivers
  • Assess cognitive and functional capacity before initiating
  • Useful in group homes, assisted living, post-acute care
  • Approved by Medicare for older adults
🤖 AID Systems — Key Trials
  • ORACL (T1D, mean age 67): AID → ↑ TIR vs sensor-augmented pump
  • AIDE T1D (≥65 years, n=82): AID → less hypoglycemia; 91% chose AID in extension phase
  • Medicare real-world (n=4,243, mean age 67): hybrid closed-loop → ↑ TIR +10%
  • Older T2D on MDI unable to self-manage: AID + home health care → TIR +27%

Lifestyle Management

13.11a Recommend healthful eating with adequate protein (≥0.8 g/kg/day) to maintain lean body mass; higher individualized amounts to regain lean body mass and function. B
13.11b Recommend regular physical activity — aerobic, weight-bearing, and resistance training as tolerated — particularly to maintain lean body mass, especially during weight loss. B
13.12 For older adults with T2D, overweight/obesity, and capacity to exercise safely: intensive lifestyle intervention (dietary changes, physical activity, weight loss 5–7%) for benefits on quality of life, mobility, and cardiometabolic risk. A
🥩 Nutrition Principles
  • Minimum protein: 0.8–1.5 g/kg/day (15–20% of calories)
  • Moderately higher intake (20–30%) may enhance satiety and support glycemic management
  • Protein supplementation + resistance exercise → ↑ muscle mass & strength in sarcopenia
  • Screen for malnutrition with MNA-SF — associated with sarcopenia and decline in ADLs, cognition, QoL
  • In PALTC: meals tailored to culture & preferences improve nutrition status and satisfaction
🏋️ Physical Activity
  • Goal in frail older adults: reduce sedentary time, prevent mobility disability, reduce frailty (LIFE study)
  • For non-frail T2D with overweight/obesity: intensive lifestyle intervention (Look AHEAD trial)
  • Look AHEAD: ↑ HDL, ↓ SBP, ↓ A1C, ↓ waist circumference, ↓ medication burden — benefits seen equally in 60–70 year age group
  • ≥10% body weight loss in year 1 → improved CV outcomes
  • Goal in frail patients: enhanced functional status (not weight loss)

Pharmacologic Therapy

13.13 Select medications with low risk of hypoglycemia in older adults with T2D, specifically those with hypoglycemia risk factors. B
13.14a Deintensify hypoglycemia-causing medications (insulin, SUs, meglitinides) or switch to low-hypoglycemia-risk class for high-risk individuals, using individualized glycemic goals. B
13.14b Deintensify diabetes medications when harms/burdens outweigh benefits, within individualized glycemic goals. E
13.14c Simplify complex treatment plans (especially insulin) to reduce hypoglycemia risk, polypharmacy, and treatment burden. B
13.14d In older adults with T2D and established or high risk of ASCVD, HF, or CKD: treatment plan should include agents that reduce CV and kidney disease risk, irrespective of glycemia. A
13.15 Consider costs of care and insurance coverage when developing treatment plans — older adults often on multiple medications and fixed incomes. B
✅ Metformin
  • Good first-line option — low hypoglycemia risk, simple dosing
  • Safe if eGFR ≥30; lower dose if eGFR 30–45; contraindicated if advanced kidney disease
  • Monitor eGFR every 3–6 months in at-risk patients
  • Caution: hypoperfusion, hypoxemia, impaired hepatic function (lactic acidosis risk)
  • Hold before iodinated contrast, hospitalization, acute illness
  • GI side effects: use extended-release formulation
  • Monitor vitamin B12 annually if on metformin >4 years
✅ GLP-1 RAs & GIP/GLP-1 RAs (Tirzepatide)
  • Highly effective, low hypoglycemia risk
  • CV benefits (MACE, stroke, MI) equal for >65 and <65 years of age
  • GLP-1 RAs associated with reduced all-cause dementia risk
  • Usable with reduced eGFR, including dialysis
  • Benefits on sleep apnea (tirzepatide), knee OA (semaglutide), MASH
  • Practical concerns: injectable (mostly), require visual/motor/cognitive skills; titrate slowly to reduce GI effects
  • Avoid/caution: unexplained weight loss, undernutrition, significant gastroparesis, recurrent ileus
  • Monitor for dehydration and excessive muscle mass loss (sarcopenia)
✅ SGLT2 Inhibitors
  • Oral — convenient for older adults
  • CV benefits in established ASCVD; HF and CKD benefits
  • Similar or greater benefits in older vs younger patients
  • Adverse effects to monitor in older adults:
  • Volume depletion — caution in frailty and orthostasis
  • Genital mycotic infections (especially women)
  • Urinary incontinence (query before & after initiation)
  • Small ↑ in UTI risk — caution with recurrent/severe UTIs
  • Euglycemic DKA — especially in PALTC (infection most common trigger)
⚠️ DPP-4 Inhibitors
  • Few side effects, minimal hypoglycemia risk
  • Weaker glucose-lowering; no CV or kidney disease benefits
  • Useful for mild hyperglycemia or when GLP-1 RA not tolerated
  • Alternative to metformin if low eGFR
  • Linagliptin: no renal dose adjustment needed
  • Cost may be a barrier
⚠️ Sulfonylureas & Meglitinides
  • Associated with hypoglycemia, bone loss, and fracture risk
  • Glyburide: avoid in older adults (prolonged action, multiple active metabolites)
  • If used, prefer shorter-acting agents (e.g. glipizide)
  • Many antimicrobials (fluoroquinolones, SMX-TMP) interact to ↑ effective SU dose → hypoglycemia risk
  • Reduce/hold during acute illness, fasting, or altered eating
  • Routinely screen for hypoglycemia in patients on SUs
⚠️ Pioglitazone
  • Low hypoglycemia risk — but use cautiously
  • Risks: HF, fluid retention, weight gain, osteoporosis, macular edema
  • Risk reduced with lower doses and in combination therapy
  • May reduce stroke risk (benefit)
💉 Insulin Therapy in Older Adults

Requirements

  • Requires good visual, motor, and cognitive ability
  • Insulin pens preferred over syringes in those with functional impairment
  • Long-acting analogs → lower risk of ED visits/hospitalizations for hypoglycemia than NPH (Medicare data)
  • Once-daily basal may be reasonable; MDI may be too complex in advanced disease or impairment

Simplification Strategy (Fig 13.3)

  • On premixed insulin: Use 70% of total dose as basal only (morning)
  • Prandial >10 units/dose: ↓ 50% + add non-insulin agent; titrate down prandials
  • Prandial ≤10 units/dose: Discontinue prandials + add non-insulin agent(s)
  • Basal titration goal: Fasting 90–150 mg/dL; adjust by ≥2 units based on pattern
  • Do NOT use rapid/short-acting insulin at bedtime
🔄 The 4-Step (4S) Pathway for Simplification/Deprescribing (Fig 13.2)
Step 1 — Assess

Identify red flags: medical events, life-altering events, change in physical/cognitive/mental health, medication red flags, unrecognized hypoglycemia, symptoms of hyperglycemia

Step 2 — Reevaluate Goals

Reevaluate glycemic goals through shared decision-making. Involve care partners. Consider 2-week CGM to evaluate glycemic patterns.

Step 3 — Deintensify/Simplify

Deintensify, simplify, or modify the diabetes treatment plan to optimize safety, minimize polypharmacy, and target disease-specific benefits

Step 4 — Reassess

Reassess treatment engagement and burdens. Consider 2-week CGM to evaluate glycemic patterns. Investigate adverse effects.

Special Considerations — Type 1 Diabetes in Older Adults

ℹ️
Growing Population Due to advances in diabetes management, people with T1D are living longer. The population of people over 65 years with T1D is growing. Many recommendations in this section apply, but T1D in older adults has unique considerations.
⚡ Key Principles — T1D in Older Adults
  • Insulin is life-preserving — never stop basal insulin (DKA risk)
  • Some form of basal insulin required even when unable to ingest meals
  • CGM approved by Medicare — critical for A1C, variability, and hypoglycemia reduction
  • Insulin delivery: injection, AID system, or pump depending on preference, capability, and circumstances
  • As complications/cognitive impairment develop → increasing importance of care partners
🏥 PALTC Settings — T1D Specific Challenges
  • Staff often less familiar with CGM devices, insulin pumps, AID systems
  • CGM feasible but requires substantial staff training
  • High prevalence of hypoglycemia in PALTC — both insulin users and sulfonylurea users
  • DKA may be mistaken for sepsis, end-organ failure, or electrolyte abnormalities
  • Individual or family may know current management better than staff
  • Education of PALTC staff on insulin dosing, pumps, and CGM is recommended (Rec 13.16)
🏠

Post-Acute & Long-Term Care (PALTC) Settings

13.16 Recommend diabetes education/training (including CGM, insulin pumps, AID systems) for staff of PALTC settings to improve management of older adults. E
13.17 People with diabetes in PALTC settings need careful assessment of mobility, mentation, medications, and management preferences to establish individualized goals and appropriate device/agent choices based on clinical and functional status. E
🍽 PALTC — Nutritional Considerations
  • Irregular/unpredictable meal intake, undernutrition, diminished appetite, impaired swallowing
  • Give insulin immediately after meals (not before) to account for variable consumption
  • Use rapid-acting (not short-acting) prandial insulin post-meal
  • Meals tailored to culture & preferences → ↑ QoL and nutrition status
  • Therapeutic nutrition plans may inadvertently ↓ caloric intake
📢 PALTC — Alert Strategy for Hypoglycemia/Hyperglycemia
  • Call immediately: BG <70 mg/dL (treat first, then call)
  • Call as soon as possible:
  • BG 70–100 mg/dL (may need plan adjustment)
  • ≥2 readings >250 mg/dL within 24h + clinical change
  • Consistently >250 mg/dL within 24h
  • Consistently >300 mg/dL over 2 consecutive days
  • Reading too high for device; individual sick/vomiting/fever/poor oral intake
  • Oral agents may achieve similar outcomes to basal insulin in T2D PALTC residents
⚠️
PALTC Assessment Frequency Federal guidelines mandate: at minimum, assessments every 30 days for first 90 days post-admission, then ≥ every 60 days. Health care professionals may adjust treatment by phone/fax/in-person if given timely notification from a standardized alert system. Persistent dysglycemia requires prompt reassessment.

End-of-Life Care

13.18 When palliative care is needed: discuss goals and intensity with person and care partners. Strict glycemic, BP, and lipid goals are not necessary. Consider deintensification/simplification and prioritize symptom management. E
13.19 Prioritize overall comfort, prevention of distressing symptoms, and preservation of quality of life and dignity as primary goals of diabetes management at end of life. C
🎯 Glycemic Goals at End of Life
  • Prevent hypoglycemia (<70 mg/dL)
  • Prevent severe hyperglycemia (>250 mg/dL when symptom burden increases)
  • No role for A1C monitoring
  • CGM may be considered when frequent finger sticks are burdensome
  • First-line pharmacotherapy: oral agents with low hypoglycemia risk → simplified basal insulin if needed
  • Avoid agents causing GI or other adverse symptoms
  • Statin withdrawal in palliative care may improve quality of life
📋 Three End-of-Life Categories
  • Stable individual: Continue previous plan; focus on hypoglycemia prevention and keeping glucose below renal threshold. No A1C.
  • Organ failure: Prevent hypoglycemia > all else. Prevent dehydration. In T1D: reduce but do not stop insulin. In T2D: reduce hypoglycemia-causing agents. Allow glucose at upper goal range.
  • Dying individual: T2D — discontinuing all medications may be reasonable (no oral intake). T1D — no consensus; small amount of basal insulin may prevent acute hyperglycemic symptoms.
📝 Advance Care Planning
  • Diabetes clinicians are well positioned to support advance care planning
  • Help document values, preferences, and goals of care
  • Research shows people with diabetes want to discuss end-of-life plans with their clinician
  • Tools: SPICT (Supportive and Palliative Care Indicators Tool); Gold Standards Framework Proactive Identification Guidance
  • Individual has the right to refuse testing and treatment
✅
Person-Centred Approach The management of diabetes in older adults at end of life necessitates a person-centred approach prioritizing individualized treatment goals, comfort, symptom management, quality of life, and dignity. Clinical decision-making should involve the individual, family, and care partners to achieve a goal-concordant care plan.

Preconception Counseling

15.1 Starting at puberty and continuing in all people with diabetes and childbearing potential, preconception counseling should be incorporated into routine diabetes care. A
15.2 Family planning should be discussed, and effective contraception (with consideration of long-acting, reversible contraception) should be prescribed and used until the individual's treatment plan and A1C are optimized for pregnancy. A

15.4 Individuals with a history of GDM should seek preconception screening for diabetes and preconception care to identify and treat hyperglycemia and prevent congenital malformations. E
📋 Preconception Glucose Goals
  • Preprandial: 80–110 mg/dL (4.4–6.1 mmol/L)

⚠️ Medications to Discontinue Before Pregnancy
  • ACE inhibitors & ARBs: Stop prior to conception or as soon as pregnancy confirmed
  • GLP-1 RAs: Discontinue before pregnancy; use contraception while on them
  • Semaglutide: Stop ≥2 months before planned pregnancy (long half-life)
  • Tirzepatide: Canadian labeling recommends ≥1 month washout
  • Statins: Stop in most cases (use pravastatin if benefit outweighs risk)
  • Fibrates, PCSK9 inhibitors, bempedoic acid: Stop before conception

  • Carbohydrate: ≥175 g/day (~35% of 2000 kcal diet)
  • Protein: ≥71 g/day
  • Fiber: 28 g/day

  • Promote nutrient-dense, higher-quality carbohydrates (improve fasting/postprandial glucose, insulin action)

  • Ketogenic diet: Severely restricts carbohydrates; increases ketone risk

  • High saturated fat diets: Worsen insulin resistance

  • Goal: ≥150 min/week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity, spread throughout the week

  • Effective types: aerobic, resistance, or both; 20–50 min/day, 2–7 days/week

  • Labetalol

  • Meta-analysis: aspirin <100 mg/day not effective in reducing preeclampsia

🔴 Critical Pregnancy Complications

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

  • Pregnancy is a ketogenic state — DKA can occur at lower blood glucose levels
  • DKA carries high risk of stillbirth
  • T1D patients should have ketone strips and education on DKA prevention/detection
  • Those unable to eat in DKA need IV fluid with dextrose + insulin drip (placenta/fetus require higher carbohydrate in 3rd trimester)

Diabetic Retinopathy

  • Rapid implementation of euglycemia in setting of retinopathy → worsening retinopathy
  • High risk of new-onset and progression in T1D and T2D during pregnancy
  • Dilated eye exams: before pregnancy, each trimester, 1 year postpartum

Hypoglycemia

  • T1D: ↑ risk in first trimester and after delivery
  • Altered counterregulatory response in pregnancy → ↓ hypoglycemia awareness
  • Educate person and family on prevention, recognition, and treatment before, during, and after pregnancy
  • Breastfeeding: ↑ risk of overnight hypoglycemia — adjust insulin doses

GDM & Early Pregnancy Hyperglycemia

ℹ️
TOBOGM Trial — Key Findings Early GDM treatment (mean 15.6 weeks) showed modest improvement in composite adverse neonatal outcome (24.9% vs 30.5%, RR 0.82 [0.68–0.98]). However: ~1/3 of those observed had spontaneous regression of GDM at 24–28 weeks with outcomes similar to those without GDM. Possible harm (↑ SGA) in those with lower glycemic OGTT values treated early. Treatment benefit not universal.
📋 Current Guidance on Early Hyperglycemia (<Overt DM)
  • Benefits of treatment remain uncertain; professional society guidance conflicts
  • Suggest: nutrition counseling + fasting glucose monitoring 3–4×/week
  • If fasting glucose predominantly ≥110 mg/dL before 15 weeks: increase to daily monitoring; intensify treatment
  • Individualized shared decision-making is reasonable for early treatment at fasting ≥110 mg/dL
  • When treatment initiated: use standard pregnancy glucose goals (Table 15.2)
⚠️ Insulin Physiology in Pregnancy
  • Early pregnancy: enhanced insulin sensitivity → ↓ glucose, ↑ hypoglycemia risk in T1D
  • From ~16 weeks: insulin resistance increases; total daily dose ↑ ~5%/week through week 36
  • Usually results in doubling of prepregnancy TDD
  • Bolus insulin takes larger proportion of TDD as pregnancy progresses
  • Requirement levels off in late third trimester
  • Rapid ↓ in requirements may indicate placental insufficiency
  • T2D: glycemic goals easier to achieve but may require concentrated insulin formulations
💡
GDM Diagnosis Timing Usual GDM diagnosis: 24–28 weeks of gestation. History of prior GDM: screen for T2D/prediabetes before 15 weeks. Individuals with known T2D history: test preconception and continue treatment with pregnancy-approved therapies.

📉 Postpartum Insulin Adjustment
  • Insulin requirements ~34% lower than prepregnancy immediately after delivery
  • Returns to prepregnancy levels over 1–2 weeks
  • Breastfeeding: ↑ risk of overnight hypoglycemia — adjust doses and monitor
  • GDM individuals: usually do not require diabetes medications postpartum
  • T1D: do not stop insulin postpartum — reduce and titrate carefully
🩺 GDM Postpartum Screening Protocol
  • At 4–12 weeks: 75-g OGTT using non-pregnancy criteria (not A1C — unreliable due to RBC changes, blood loss, preceding 3-month glucose profile)
  • If unable to tolerate OGTT: A1C at 6–12 months postpartum may be considered
  • A1C 5.7% threshold: 80% sensitivity, 80.8% specificity for detecting 2-hr glucose ≥200 mg/dL
  • Then: Screen every 1–3 years lifelong with any recommended glycemic test
📊 Lifetime T2D Risk After GDM
  • Estimated lifetime risk: 50–60%
  • 10-fold ↑ risk vs those without GDM
  • Linear increase: ~20% at 10 yrs → 30% at 20 yrs → 40% at 30 yrs → 50% at 40 yrs → 60% at 50 yrs
  • Two GDM pregnancies: hazard ratios even higher than one GDM pregnancy
  • Prevention: lifestyle intervention and/or metformin reduce progression ~35–40% over 10 years (NNT = 5–6)
  • Weight management after GDM: critical — T2D risk 18% higher per unit BMI increase

8.7 Nutrition, physical activity, and behavioral therapy are recommended for T2D + overweight/obesity to achieve weight and health goals. B
8.8a Intensive programs with ≥16 sessions in 6 months, focusing on nutrition, physical activity, and behavioral strategies to achieve a 500–750 kcal/day energy deficit, should be recommended for weight loss when available. A
8.8b If access is limited, consider alternative structured programs (remote, telehealth, mobile app) delivering nutrition, physical activity, and behavioral counseling. E
8.9 Nutrition recommendations should be individualized to the person's preferences and nutritional needs. Use plans that create an energy deficit while following general nutritional guidance. A
8.10 Consider systemic, structural, cultural, and socioeconomic factors affecting nutrition patterns — including food insecurity, access to healthful foods, and social determinants of health. C
8.11 For those who achieve weight loss goals, provide ongoing support and long-term (≥1 year) maintenance programs with monthly contact, frequent self-monitoring (weekly or more), and ≥200–300 min/week physical activity. A
8.12 Very-low-calorie diets (800–1,000 kcal/day) should be prescribed only to carefully selected individuals by trained practitioners in medical settings with close monitoring. Long-term weight maintenance strategies must be integrated. B

🍽️ Behavioral Intervention Design (DPP / Look AHEAD Model)
  • ≥16 sessions in first 6 months
  • Focus: durable nutritional changes + physical activity + behavioral strategies
  • Energy deficit: 500–750 kcal/day
  • Women: ~1,200–1,500 kcal/day; Men: ~1,500–1,800 kcal/day (adjusted for body weight)
  • Can be face-to-face or remote; individual or group
  • Look AHEAD: ~50% of participants lost/maintained ≥5% weight over 8 years; those who lost ≥10% at 1 year had 21% ↓ mortality risk
⚠️ Very-Low-Calorie Diet (VLCD)
  • 800–1,000 kcal/day
  • High-protein foods and meal replacement products
  • DiRECT trial: structured VLCD → increased pace and magnitude of weight loss vs standard care
  • Restrict to: Carefully selected patients (e.g., requiring weight loss before surgery)
  • Risks: Electrolyte abnormalities, severe fatigue, cardiac arrhythmias
  • Duration: Generally up to 3 months; must include long-term maintenance plan
🏃 Physical Activity
  • Encourage primarily for overall health, not just weight loss
  • Improves quality of life, cardiorespiratory fitness, glycemia; reduces mortality
  • Goal: 150 min/week of activities the person enjoys
  • Weight maintenance programs: recommend 200–300 min/week
  • Encourage activities they enjoy — adherence depends on enjoyment
🧬 Nutritional Monitoring During Weight Loss
  • Consider multivitamin/mineral if <1,200 kcal/day, or excluding micronutrient-rich food groups
  • Screen for micronutrient deficiencies if >20% or rapid (>4%/month) weight loss
  • Key nutrients: iron, calcium, magnesium, zinc, vitamins A, D, E, K, B1, B12, C
  • Protein: Optimize intake + resistance training to preserve lean mass
  • Adequate fiber and water to prevent constipation (especially on VLCDs)
  • Referral to registered dietitian nutritionist recommended
🚫
Low-Carbohydrate Diets A 2022 meta-analysis (3–12 months to 2 years, people with overweight/obesity with or without diabetes) showed no greater benefits for low-carbohydrate (>50–150 g/day or <45% total energy) vs balanced carbohydrate (45–65% total energy) for weight or A1C reduction (mean A1C difference: −0.14%). ✅ Predictors of T2D Remission After Surgery
  • Younger age
  • Shorter diabetes duration (especially <8 years)
  • Lower severity: better glycemic control, not using insulin
  • Greater baseline visceral fat area (especially in Asian Americans)

  • Majority maintain substantial glycemic improvement from baseline for at least 5–15 years
⚠️ Long-Term Complications of Metabolic Surgery
  • Nutritional deficiencies: vitamin and mineral deficiencies, anemia (require lifelong supplementation)
  • Osteoporosis
  • Dumping syndrome: diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, palpitations, fatigue 10–30 min after meal; usually not hypoglycemia at symptom time
  • Post-surgery hypoglycemia: usually >1 year post-op; reactive hypoglycemia 1–3 hrs after high-carb meal; manage with dietary modification + CGM ± acarbose/diazoxide/octreotide
  • Psychosocial: ↑ substance use, depression/anxiety, suicidal ideation — monitor and refer
  • Perioperative mortality: 0.1–0.5%; major complications 2–6% (comparable to cholecystectomy)

  • GLP-1 RAs pre-surgery: In BMI >50 kg/m², associated with significant weight loss without increase in complications or delay to surgery
  • GLP-1 RAs post-surgery: Can augment initial weight loss or treat plateau; effective for post-surgery weight recurrence (alternative to revisional surgery)
  • Provide guidance on GLP-1 RA discontinuation prior to surgery to mitigate aspiration risk with anesthesia
🏆
Benefits of Metabolic Surgery Beyond Weight Loss ↓ microvascular disease · ↑ quality of life · ↓ cancer risk · ↓ cardiovascular events and risk factors · ↓ all-cause mortality · ↑ liver outcomes (MASH/MASLD) · ↓ sleep apnea · Superior glycemic management vs non-surgical intervention in T2D + obesity.

Obesity Management in Type 1 Diabetes

8.29 Apply obesity management strategies used in the general adult population, including GLP-1 RA–based therapy B and metabolic surgery, C to adults with T1D who have obesity (BMI ≥30 kg/m², or ≥27.5 in Asian American individuals). Shared decision-making should inform individualized care.
📊 Epidemiology of Obesity in T1D
  • Overweight prevalence: 30–40%
  • Obesity prevalence: 15–30% — comparable to general population
  • Obesity in T1D → ↑ CVD burden and ↑ microvascular complications vs T1D without obesity
  • GLP-1 RAs/dual GIP/GLP-1 RAs prescribed in ~6.5% of T1D patients in 2023 (rising trend)
⚠️ Special Risks: GLP-1 RAs in T1D
  • ADJUNCT ONE/TWO (liraglutide 1.8 mg): ~6% weight reduction but hypoglycemia ↑ 20–30% and hyperglycemia with ketosis doubled
  • Appetite suppression + ↓caloric intake + ↓insulin requirements → ketosis risk
  • Before initiating: detailed counseling on anticipated ↓ insulin requirements, ketone monitoring, maintaining critical carbohydrate intake, sick-day rules
  • Titration: must be more cautious than T2D protocols; close monitoring of insulin requirements and hypoglycemia frequency
  • Discontinuation in >50% of patients with obesity at 1 year → variable ↑ in insulin requirements
🤖 GLP-1 RA + Automated Insulin Delivery (AID)
  • Requires periodic reassessment of AID settings
  • Deintensification of insulin-to-carbohydrate ratios and sensitivity factor expected during titration
  • Delayed gastric emptying → may require modification of mealtime insulin dosing to beginning of or just after meal
  • Goal: reduce hypoglycemia risk and avoid prolonged insulin delivery suspensions that may predispose to ketosis
🚫 Caution: Conditions Discouraging GLP-1 RA Initiation in T1D
  • Preexisting gastroparesis
  • Hypoglycemia unawareness
  • Recent episode of DKA or euglycemic ketoacidosis
✅ Comorbidities That Favor GLP-1 RA in T1D
  • Obstructive sleep apnea
  • MASH/MASLD
  • Coronary artery disease
🔴
Metabolic Surgery in T1D — Key Safety Concerns Average BMI reduction from ~41 to ~31 kg/m² post-surgery; however, A1C effects are inconsistent (one study: ↑ A1C at 5 years). DKA rate: 15–20% — often in first days post-surgery with ↓carbohydrate intake and ↓insulin doses. Enhanced hypoglycemia risk. Dumping syndrome is particularly challenging to manage in T1D. A multidisciplinary approach with careful evaluation and extensive counseling is required.

🔑 Key Risk Factors for Microvascular Complications
  • Chronic hyperglycemia — the most important modifiable risk factor (metabolic memory effect)

  • Intensive therapy (A1C ~7%) vs conventional (A1C ~9%) in T1D showed lasting protection

  • Mechanisms: glycation, lipoxidation, inflammation, apoptosis, epigenetic changes
  • Implication: early and sustained glycemic control matters most — start early

Diabetic Retinopathy

🔬 Pathophysiology & Staging
  • Earliest: Microaneurysms
  • Progression: Retinal hemorrhages → ischemia → neovascularization
  • Proliferative DR (PDR): Neovascularization in response to ischemia; risk of vitreous hemorrhage and tractional retinal detachment
  • Diabetic Macular Edema (DME): Can occur at any stage — retinal vascular leakage of fluid/exudates
  • Glaucoma and cataracts also occur earlier and more frequently in diabetes
⚠️ GLP-1 RA & Eye Health — Key Points
  • GLP-1 RAs (liraglutide, semaglutide, dulaglutide) associated with mild worsening of DR in some RCTs
  • Rapid A1C reductions with GLP-1 RAs may cause initial DR worsening — assess retinopathy before intensifying therapy
  • Matched cohort data links GLP-1 RAs with nonarteritic anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (NAION) and AMD — limited evidence, further studies needed
  • Possible benefits: lower intraocular pressure, reduced glaucoma risk with GLP-1 RA use
📋 Screening Recommendations
12.1–2 Implement strategies to reach glycemic, blood pressure, and lipid goals to reduce the risk or slow the progression of diabetic retinopathy. A
12.3 Type 1 diabetes: Initial dilated and comprehensive eye exam 5 years after diagnosis by ophthalmologist or optometrist. B
12.4 Type 2 diabetes: Initial dilated exam at time of diagnosis (may have had years of undetected hyperglycemia). B
12.5 No retinopathy + glycemic goals met: screening every 1–2 years after ≥1 normal exams. Any DR present: repeat at least annually. Progressing or sight-threatening: more frequent ophthalmology. B
12.6 Retinal photography with remote reading or FDA-approved AI algorithms are appropriate screening strategies. Must provide pathways for referral when indicated. B
12.7–8 Pregnancy: Counsel individuals with preexisting T1D or T2D on DR risk. Eye exam before pregnancy, in first trimester, and every trimester + 1 year postpartum as indicated. Gestational DM does NOT require eye exam. B

Diabetic Neuropathy

ℹ️
Key Points on Diabetic Neuropathy Diabetic neuropathy is a diagnosis of exclusion. Up to 50% of DPN cases are asymptomatic. Cardiovascular autonomic neuropathy (CAN) is associated with mortality independent of other CV risk factors. No treatment reverses neuronal loss — management focuses on prevention and symptom control.
🔬 Screening for DPN
12.17 All people with diabetes should be assessed for DPN starting at diagnosis of T2D and 5 years after diagnosis of T1D, then at least annually. B
12.18 Assessment: careful history + temperature or pinprick (small fiber) + vibration with 128-Hz tuning fork (large fiber). All patients: annual 10-g monofilament testing for ulceration/amputation risk. B
🫀 Autonomic Neuropathy Screening
12.19 Assess for autonomic neuropathy from diagnosis of T2D and 5 years after T1D diagnosis, annually, and with evidence of other microvascular complications — especially CKD and DPN. E
  • Ask about: Orthostatic dizziness, syncope, early satiety, erectile dysfunction, changes in sweating, dry cracked skin in extremities
  • Examine for: Orthostatic hypotension, resting tachycardia, peripheral dryness/cracking
🔍 Differential Diagnosis of Neuropathy in Diabetes

Always consider non-diabetic causes — these may be treatable:

Metabolic / Nutritional Vitamin B12 deficiency (esp. with metformin), acquired copper deficiency (post-metabolic surgery), hypothyroidism, kidney disease
Toxic / Drug-related Alcohol, chemotherapy, other neurotoxic medications
Other Causes Malignancy (myeloma, bronchogenic Ca), HIV, hepatitis C, CIDP, inherited neuropathies, vasculitis

12.20 Optimize glucose (prevent/delay DPN in T1D A; slow progression in T2D C). Optimize weight, BP, and lipids to reduce DPN risk. B

🩺 Orthostatic Hypotension Management
  • Goal: minimize postural symptoms — not restore normotension
  • Non-pharmacologic: Adequate salt intake, avoid aggravating medications, compressive garments, exercise, fluid/salt repletion
  • Supine hypertension management: Guanfacine/clonidine, shorter-acting calcium blockers (isradipine), β-blockers (atenolol, metoprolol tartrate), or enalapril as alternative
  • FDA-approved agents: Midodrine, Droxidopa
🫁 Gastroparesis Management
  • Small-particle diet → some symptom relief
  • Withdraw offending drugs: opioids, anticholinergics, TCAs, GLP-1 RAs, pramlintide (balance benefits vs risks for GLP-1 RAs)
  • Metoclopramide: Only FDA-approved prokinetic — use restricted to <12 weeks due to extrapyramidal risks (tardive dyskinesia, acute dystonia, drug-induced parkinsonism)
  • Domperidone (outside US) and short-term erythromycin (tachyphylaxis limits use)
  • Gastric electrical stimulation: FDA-approved device but limited evidence of efficacy

Diabetic Foot Care

🔴
Foot Complications = Major Source of Morbidity & Mortality Foot ulcerations, infections, and amputations result from peripheral neuropathy, PAD, and foot deformities acting together. Infection is usually the final precipitating cause of lower-extremity amputations. People with diabetes and a major lower-limb amputation have 67% 5-year mortality (vs 57% in non-diabetes).
📋 Foot Care Recommendations
12.23 Perform a comprehensive foot evaluation at least annually to identify risk factors for ulcers and amputations. A
12.24 Exam must include: skin inspection, foot deformities, neurological assessment (10-g monofilament or Ipswich touch test + ≥1 additional test), vascular assessment including pedal pulses. B
12.25 Individuals with sensory loss or prior ulceration/amputation: feet inspected at every visit. A
12.26 Obtain history: ulceration, amputation, Charcot foot, angioplasty/vascular surgery, smoking, retinopathy, renal disease. Assess current neuropathy and vascular symptoms. B
12.27 PAD screening: lower-extremity pulses, capillary refill, rubor on dependency, pallor on elevation, venous filling time. Leg fatigue, claudication, rest pain, or ↓ pedal pulses → refer for ankle-brachial index with toe pressures. B
12.28 Interprofessional team facilitated by a podiatrist recommended for foot ulcers and high-risk feet (dialysis, Charcot foot, prior ulcer/amputation, PAD). B
12.29 Smokers with prior lower-extremity complications, LOPS, structural abnormalities, or PAD → refer to foot care specialists for lifelong surveillance. Provide smoking cessation counseling. B
12.30–31 Foot self-care education for all patients with diabetes. Specialized therapeutic footwear recommended for high-risk patients (LOPS, deformities, ulcers, callus, poor circulation, prior amputation). B
12.32 Chronic DFUs failing optimal standard care: consider adjunctive RCT-proven advanced agents (negative-pressure wound therapy, skin substitutes, topical oxygen therapy). A

🔎 LOPS Assessment — Clinical Tests
  • 10-g monofilament: Primary LOPS screen + 1 additional test required (monofilament alone detects more advanced DPN but misses early disease)
  • Ipswich Touch Test: Preferred in UK and several countries; finger touch to detect LOPS
  • Additional tests: Pinprick, temperature perception, ankle reflexes, vibratory perception (128-Hz tuning fork)
  • Absent monofilament sensation + one other abnormal test = LOPS confirmed
  • Electrophysiology, MRI spine, neurology referral rarely needed unless atypical (asymmetric, acute/subacute, motor involvement)
🩸 PAD Evaluation
  • Screen all patients >50 years with diabetes via non-invasive arterial studies; if normal, repeat every 5 years
  • Doppler US with pulse volume recordings (ankle-brachial indices may be inaccurate due to non-compressible vessels in diabetes)
  • Toe systolic BP <30 mmHg → suggests PAD and inability to heal ulcers; refer for formal vascular evaluation + angiography
  • WIfI staging (Wound, Ischemia, foot Infection) used for PAD severity, amputation risk, and DFU healing prediction
💊 Drug Effects on Lower-Extremity Outcomes
  • GLP-1 RAs (esp. semaglutide): May reduce lower-extremity amputations, DFUs, and all-cause mortality vs SGLT2i; STRIDE trial: semaglutide improved walking distance and reduced composite adverse limb events in PAD (Fontaine IIa)
  • Oral semaglutide (SOUL trial): Significantly lower rate of major adverse limb events
  • Canagliflozin (CANVAS): Associated with increased lower-limb amputation risk (mostly toes) — consider stopping if significant lower-limb complication develops until resolved
🏥 Diabetic Foot Ulcer (DFU) Management
📋
5 Basic Principles of Ulcer Treatment (1) Offloading / pressure relief; (2) Debridement of hyperkeratotic/necrotic tissue; (3) Revascularization of ischemic wounds when necessary; (4) Management of infection — soft tissue or bone; (5) Wound-appropriate topical dressings
🔬 Infection Assessment
  • Not all ulcers are infected — look for clinical signs or exposed/probed bone
  • Tissue cultures + X-ray (bone erosions/osteomyelitis, abscess, gas)
  • MRI most useful for extent of bone infection and preoperative planning
  • Abscesses → drain promptly (chairside or OR depending on severity)
  • Necrotizing soft tissue infection / wet gangrene = surgical emergency → immediate surgical referral for wide incision/drainage ± amputation
  • Osteomyelitis: bone cultures + histopathology; conservative, surgical, or combined approach (IWGDF/IDSA combined guideline)
🌀 Advanced Wound Therapies

Consider if ≥50% wound area reduction not achieved after 4 weeks of standard care:

  • Negative-pressure wound therapy (NPWT): Useful for deep/large wounds and wound preparation for grafts — RCT evidence
  • Topical oxygen therapy: Multiple high-quality RCTs + systematic reviews support efficacy in healing chronic DFUs at 12 weeks; home-based, 3 device types available — strong evidence base
  • Bioengineered skin substitutes / acellular matrix: Apligraf, Dermagraft (FDA-approved); used for chronic superficial ulcers
  • Growth factors: Becaplermin (FDA-approved)
  • Hyperbaric oxygen: Limited RCT evidence; may benefit selected chronic neuroischemic ulcers; no benefit in non-ischemic DFUs
  • Electrical stimulation, ultrasound, shockwave therapy — mostly retrospective/poor-quality RCTs
🦴 Charcot Neuroarthropathy
⚠️
Red Flag: Warm, Swollen, Red Foot in Neuropathy Any patient with neuropathy presenting with a warm, swollen, erythematous foot with or without trauma and no open ulceration → thorough workup for Charcot neuroarthropathy. Foot/ankle X-rays mandatory. Early diagnosis and treatment is paramount — delays lead to deformity, instability, ulceration, and amputation.
  • Initial management: Total non-weight-bearing + urgent referral to foot care specialist
  • Surgical reconstruction for complex deformities — high success rates and limb salvage reported; requires experienced diabetic foot surgeon
